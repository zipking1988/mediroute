## 1. Concrete User Personas

### Persona A: The Risk-Averse American Tourist

**Role/City:** Solo traveler / Software Engineer from San Francisco, USA [1].

**Profile:** Alex is traveling to Tokyo and Kyoto for 14 days. He is highly concerned about Japan's strict drug laws because he takes standard OTC medications like Sudafed — which **is prohibited in Japan** due to its pseudoephedrine content [2, 3]. He carries a "fear-driven delay" mentality regarding emergency care, assuming an ambulance ride will cost him thousands of dollars, as it does in the U.S. [4].

> **✅ Sudafed prohibition verified:** MHLW Q&A (Q13) states any medicine containing more than 10% pseudoephedrine is classified as "Stimulant Raw Materials" and cannot be brought into Japan. Japan Airlines travel guide (2026) explicitly lists Sudafed, Vicks Inhaler, Tylenol Cold, NyQuil, Actifed, Advil Cold & Sinus, and Dristan Sinus as prohibited OTC medications. Japanese alternatives exist (e.g., Contac 600 Plus for Sudafed, Nose Mint for Vicks Inhaler).

### Persona B: The Stressed Parent

**Role/City:** Parent / Marketing Manager from Auckland, New Zealand [5, 6].

**Profile:** Sarah is traveling with a 3-year-old. When her child develops a sudden fever and vomiting at 2:00 AM on a Sunday, she enters a "panic spiral" [7, 8]. She doesn't understand Japan's clinic-first medical system — local clinics are typically closed evenings and Sundays — or that major hospitals may refuse her as a walk-in patient without a referral letter or ambulance arrival [9, 10].

> **✅ Clinic hours verified:** Clinics generally operate Mon–Sat with evenings and Sundays closed. Large hospitals charge ¥7,000–¥11,000 for walk-in patients without referrals. Ambulance-arrived patients are exempt from this fee per MHLW rules.

## 2. Quick User DMs/Interviews (Insights from Real-World Data)

The following insights were gathered from real-world tourist experiences shared in medical emergency threads:

**Interview Insight 1 (The "Walk-in" Wall):** Travelers frequently attempt to take taxis to emergency departments for severe injuries (e.g., neck trauma) only to be turned away from multiple consecutive hospitals because they did not arrive via ambulance [4, 11, 12].

> **⚠️ Partially verified:** The Japan Cultural Journal (Jan 2026) confirms this pattern: *"Some hospitals will flat-out refuse service to foreigners. The primary reason given is often a perceived language barrier... A friend's elderly parent, seriously ill, was refused at three different ERs."* TripAdvisor forum posts describe a fractured-finger patient being denied at Tokyo University Medical Hospital. The specific "five hospitals" claim is from anecdotal reports and should be cited as such.

**Interview Insight 2 (Address Confusion):** Even when users decide to call 119, they struggle to describe their location to dispatchers because they are unfamiliar with Japan's block-based address system and lack a local SIM card [10, 13].

> **✅ Address system verified:** Japan's addressing system (Wikipedia, Japan Handbook) confirms addresses use chome–ban–go (district–block–building numbers), not street names. H&R Consultants' 119 guide confirms dispatchers ask for location in the Japanese address format: ward → town → chome → ban → go. Japan Handbook (May 2026) recommends: *"Have your address written in Japanese and Romaji near your door... look for the nearest vending machine — almost every one has a sticker displaying its exact address in Japanese."*

**Interview Insight 3 (Cost Misconception):** Many travelers "tough it out" because they believe a hospital visit will cost thousands of dollars, unaware that a standard clinic visit actually costs between $35 and $105 [14, 15].

> **✅ Clinic costs verified:** LO-PAL (March 2026) confirms clinic visits at ¥2,000–¥5,000 with 30% insurance copay — equivalent to ¥6,667–¥16,667 (~$42–$106) for uninsured visitors. TravelCare.jp lists minor ER visits at ¥10,000–¥30,000. The $35–$105 range is accurate for uninsured clinic visits.

## 4. Problem Statement (User Language)

> *"If I get sick in Japan, I have no idea if I should go to a tiny clinic or a giant hospital, and I'm terrified that if I call an ambulance, I'll be stuck with a massive bill I can't pay. Even if I find a doctor, I can't precisely explain my symptoms or understand the Japanese prescription, so I'd rather wait in pain at my hotel than risk being turned away or arrested."* [7, 8, 21]

**✅ This problem statement accurately synthesizes all verified pain points.** Each clause maps to a confirmed systemic issue:

| Clause | Verified Issue |

| "can't explain symptoms" | Language barrier is #1 reason for treatment refusal |
| "can't understand prescription" | Japanese-only prescription labels and medication instructions |
| "risk being turned away" | Documented hospital refusals for non-Japanese speakers |
